# Panel Discussion
*After individual reviews — open floor*

---

**Shreya:** Before we start on fixes — I want to say something. The copy on this site is genuinely better than most of what I see. The voice is real. The hero headline earns its place. That's not the problem. The problem is that the site is a very well-written CV for a person who doesn't fully exist on the page yet. No face, no proof, no external validation. All the positioning work is done. The credibility architecture is missing.

**Rahul:** Agreed. I don't want to be brutal for the sake of it but I have to be honest — I spent three minutes looking for something to attach trust to and found nothing. Not one number. Not one client type. Not one outcome. Advisory is a trust product and trust requires proof. Right now this is a very attractive brochure for a practice that hasn't established its track record yet — at least not publicly.

**Meera:** I'm the person this is built for and I want to be clear: I almost contacted her. The headline got me. The About opening got me. Then I hit the wall — five paragraphs of positioning with no evidence in them — and I slowed down. The thing that stopped me was the question I couldn't answer: "Has she done this before, for someone like me, at the scale I'm dealing with?" The site doesn't answer that. It's all framing, no substrate.

**Ankit:** The conversion mechanics are broken in a specific way. The site is optimised for warm referral traffic — someone who's already been told about Priya, lands here, and is looking to confirm what they've already heard. For that visitor, this works. For cold or semi-warm traffic — LinkedIn share, search, someone who found the URL — there's no social proof, no secondary CTA, and no lower-friction path than a full contact form submission. The site has one conversion mechanism and it's the hardest one.

**Marcus:** I'm going to bring us back to something more concrete. Two things: one is the placeholder data — `hello@priya.com`, LinkedIn going to the homepage — these are launch blockers, full stop. Any technically literate visitor who inspects these will clock them in 30 seconds. The second is the favicon. I know it sounds small but in 2025, a professional services site with no favicon is like showing up to a client meeting without a business card. The diamond mark is right there. It takes 20 minutes to generate the favicon.

---

**Shreya:** The proof problem is the thread that runs through everything everyone's saying. Rahul can't refer her because there's nothing to attach an endorsement to. Meera won't convert because she needs evidence of prior similar work. Ankit can't build a conversion path because there's nothing to put in the middle of the funnel. I'm the one saying the voice is right, but even I'm saying it doesn't fully land without a human attached to it.

**Meera:** Can I raise the "Selected engagements only" pill? I had a reaction to it that I think is worth naming. It's meant to signal exclusivity. For me, in the moment, it added friction — it made me feel like I was applying to her rather than exploring whether we could work together. That might be the right signal for someone who already knows her reputation. For a first-time visitor, it can read as arrogance without the foundation to back it up.

**Ankit:** It's a sequencing problem. Scarcity signals work when credibility is already established. You put "selected engagements" on the Andreessen Horowitz website because everyone already knows who they are. On a site where you're still building awareness, the same signal reads differently. It's not wrong — it's premature.

**Rahul:** The about section length is also worth flagging. Five paragraphs of narrative is a lot to front-load before you've given the reader any proof that the narrative is backed up. I'd flip the sequence: one short intro, one proof element, then the extended narrative for those who want it.

**Marcus:** On the design side — the body copy contrast (`#8c877e` on dark backgrounds) doesn't pass WCAG AA. That's not just an accessibility issue; on mobile in low light it's actually hard to read. And the mobile nav logo — the SVG at 34px height with a 7.5px "Technology Advisor" subtext renders at roughly 3px effective size on a phone. Nobody reads that. Simplify the mobile logo to the mark only.

**Shreya:** The five differentiators section title — "Why Priya Saira Mistry" — is the most self-conscious piece of copy on the site. It's the only place where the voice drops into something that feels slightly awkward. "How I work" or "The approach" would be less stiff.

---

## Points of consensus across all five reviewers

1. **No proof** — the single biggest gap. No outcomes, no case types, no numbers, no client voice. This is the root cause of most conversion failure on this site.
2. **No photo** — Shreya, Marcus, Meera, and Rahul all flagged independently. Not optional for a personal advisory practice.
3. **Broken contact details** — universally flagged. Not live until these are real.
4. **No favicon** — Marcus flagged. Minor but visible.
5. **Secondary CTA missing** — Ankit flagged. The site has one conversion path and it's too heavy for cold traffic.
6. **The scarcity signals are premature** — Meera and Ankit both reacted to this.
7. **Services describe deliverables, not outcomes** — Shreya and Rahul both flagged.

## Points of disagreement

- **The dark design**: Marcus thinks it's right for the audience. Ankit wonders if it's slightly harder to scan on mobile in bright daylight. Panel consensus: it's the right call, execution just needs tightening.
- **Copy length**: Rahul wants shorter. Shreya thinks the length is right once proof elements are woven in. Panel consensus: don't cut — restructure. Lead with proof, then depth.
- **"Selected engagements only"**: Meera would remove it at this stage. Shreya would keep it. Panel consensus: keep it, but add substance around it so it reads as selective rather than defensive.
